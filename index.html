Swivel


Swivel is a travel app that allows users to easily collaborate on paired and group travel plans.



Role


  • UX Designer

  • 3 weeks

    • Tools

    • The Problem

    Travelers find it stressful to plan paired and group travel as it is difficult to ensure other’s expectations for accommodations and activities are being met.

    The Solution

    Swivel is a collaborative travel planning app that allows users to easily add guests to their trip, and then browse, vote on potential accommodations and activities and view their collective matches.

    User Research


    User Interviews

    I partnered with a classmate, Nicole Tierney for the user research portion of this case study. We began our research with a proto persona to act as a hypothesis. We then conducted 6 user interviews to determine how people plan for a vacation.

    User Persona

    Based on the feedback from our user interviews we developed a user persona that represents the needs of our users. Meet Marion.

    Definition and Ideation


    User Insight

    During our user interviews, we discovered that 83% of our participants find meeting the expectations of their fellow travelers to be the most difficult part about paired/group travel planning. Therefore, I believe that group travelers desire a less stressful way to collaborate with their travel companions and that I might help if I develop an app that facilitates decision-making for travel planning. How might we simplify the planning collaboration process so that each our of users feel represented in their group’s choices for travel accommodations and activities?


    Problem Statement

    Travelers like Marion need an easier way to make decisions about travel accommodations and activities that enables streamlined collaboration between multiple people.


    I Like, I Wish, What If

    I then brainstormed using the “I like, I wish, what if” ideation model with our user persona as my north star. Myself and one of the users I previously interviewed during the research process then selected the concept in each column that we liked most.

    Because our selections didn’t overlap, I went back through each concept and grouped them by topic or commonality. My interviewee and I then picked two groups each that spoke to us. Organization Ease and Recommendations were selected by both parties. Next I used a Feature Prioritization Matrix to identify which features to focus on for my initial prototype.




    Value Proposition

    Swivel is a travel planning app that allows users to browse and vote on potential accommodations and activities with their chosen travel companion(s).


    User Storyboard

    I then developed a User Scenario, then a User Journey Map, which in turn developed into a Storyboard.

    Marion is planning a trip for a group of her friends. She wants to make sure everyone's needs and expectations are met. She also wants to make sure everyone feels heard in the planning process.

    In comes the app, Swivel. Swivel allows each tripmate to swipe through their travel options in order to vote yes or no.

    Prototyping


    Competitor Analysis

    Before I developed my initial sketches and wireframes, I conducted a competitor analysis to see how other travel apps solve similar user problems. I included Wanderlog, Travefy, TripIt and Poll for All on my competitor analysis.

    Wanderlog and Travefy were more closely related to the app I was trying to develop. However, their primary features were organizing and sharing itineraries. Neither app had a solution for making initial group travel decisions.



    User Flow

    Next I developed a User Flow to inform my initial sketches and wireframes for Swivel. My user flow takes the user through onboarding, selecting a travel destination and dates, adding trip mates, applying filters and viewing results.





    Sketch to Wireframes

    I made my initial sketches on paper just to get my ideas down and make sure the User Flow works as expected.

    I turned those paper sketches into a clickable prototype in Figma. Then I iterated and created new digital wireframes in Figma.








    Testing and Iteration


    User Testing

    I tested my digital wireframe with 6 users; iterating between each interview based on the user’s comments and interactions with the prototype.


    Objectives:

    A. Can users add guests to their trip?

    B. Are users able to vote yes or no on results and do they find value in this feature?


    Tasks:

    1. Search for accommodations in Denver, Colorado.

    2. Add a guest to your trip

    3. Apply filters and vote on the results


    Testing Results

    All 6 users were able to complete all 3 tasks with ease. However, I still received some great feedback and was able to iterate based on their interactions.

    Although the 1st user completed each task with ease, I sensed they didn’t understand that they are browsing and voting with their trip mates. As I intended this to be the primary feature of the app, I continued to iterate the prototype until users highlighted the app’s features without prompting.

    Iteration


    I made several iterations between the 2nd and current version of my match screen page. Updates include moving non-matches to the bottom of the screen, lowering the opacity of non-matches so not to pull focus from matches, and putting the match details within an accordion section.

    I also added a success message after a trip mate is added to further confirm to the user that they are planning along with a friend.
